About

Hello, my name is Tiffany Liou, and I am a prospective applicant to PhD programs! I have recently received my Master’s in Physics from Northwestern University, where I worked with CIERA Postdoctoral Fellow Dr. Xinfeng Xu and Professor Allison Strom to explore scaling relationships between warm-ionized galactic outflows and their host galaxies at Cosmic Noon (z~2-3) using the Keck Baryonic Structure Survey and Keck Lyman Continuum Survey. Before experiencing the seasons, I earned my Bachelor’s degree in Physics with a Specialization in Astrophysics from UC San Diego. There, I completed my undergraduate honors thesis on using Markov chain Monte Carlo methods to infer properties of dark matter subhalos from perturbed signatures in stellar streams.

My research interest is in using multi-wavelength observations and models to understand the formation and evolution of galaxies. I am motivated by the following driving questions:

  1. What were the environments surrounding the first galaxies?
  2. How do galaxies regulate their growth?
  3. How did the universe get reionized?

Research

I am interested in using multi-wavelength observational surveys to study galaxy evolution and formation. Over the years, I have participated in a range of research projects that have further solidified my scientific interests and my goal of attending graduate school.

Galactic Outflows at Cosmic Noon

The evolution and growth of galaxies are closely linked to feedback mechanisms, such as galactic outflows. Active galactic nuclei, supernova explosions, and intense star formation inject energy and momentum into the interstellar medium (ISM), driving large-scale outflows. Cosmic Noon (z~1-3) marks the peak of star formation and black hole accretion in the universe. This epoch offers a unique opportunity to study feedback effects.

As a Master’s student at Northwestern University, I worked with Dr. Xinfeng Xu and Dr. Allison Strom to study scaling relations between galactic outflows and their host galaxies at Cosmic Noon. Using deep spectroscopic data from the Keck Baryonic Structure Survey (KBSS) and Keck Lyman Continuum Survey (KLCS), I obtained robust measurements of galactic outflows for 98 galaxies using Hα and [O III] λ5007. In my Master’s thesis and a paper in preparation, I show that outflows beyond z~2 have higher velocities and can remove more material for fixed stellar mass, star formation rate (SFR), and SFR density.

Inference of Dark Matter Subhalos

Stellar streams are remnants of tidally stripped globular clusters or dwarf galaxies. The thin, ribbon-like structure of stellar streams makes them sensitive to changes in gravitational potential and small perturbations. Because dark matter interacts gravitationally, stellar streams are the ideal candidates for investigating the properties of smaller dark matter substructures, such as subhalos.

In 2022, I participated in a summer internship at Carnegie Observatories, where I worked with Dr. Josh Simon to understand the features in stellar streams that indicate subhalo perturbations. By analyzing N-body simulations of GD-1 and Pal-5-like streams, I found that subhalo flybys induce gaps in stream density, increase velocity dispersion, and increase stream width.

I continued working on stellar streams at UC San Diego for my undergraduate thesis under the guidance of Dr. Tongyan Lin and Dr. Mukul Sholapurkar. Here, I contributed to a coding pipeline to simulate subhalo-stream interactions and infer subhalo properties (mass, size, 3-D velocity, and impact parameter) using Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

Rest-UV Properties of Lyman-alpha Emitters

Lyman-alpha Emitters (LAEs) are galaxies that have strong Lyα emissions and have Lyα equivalent widths EW > 20Å. The environments surrounding high-redshift LAEs are exciting because the distant universe is denser and richer in neutral hydrogen. In this environment, Lyα photons would have been scattered out of the line of sight of our telescopes. However, Lyα photons escaped from neutral hydrogen and likely contributed to the reionization of the universe.

In 2023, I worked with Dr. Nimisha Kumari at the Space Telescope Science Institute as part of the Society of Physics Students internship. I analyzed scaling relationships of 98 LAEs using rest-UV emission lines from the MUSE Hubble Ultra Deep Field DR2 (Lyα, CIV λ1549,50, HeII λ1640, and CIII] λ1907,9). I found an anti-correlation between stellar mass and UV magnitude, a strong correlation between stellar mass and SFR, and no major redshift evolution in these scaling relations. Comparing my LAE sample to the local star-forming main sequence (Renzini et al. 2015) and local high-redshift analogs from the COS Legacy Archive Spectroscopy Survey (Berg et al. 2022), I found that my sample is not comparable to local analogs. This result prompts me to investigate the similarities and differences between high-redshift galaxies and local analogs. In the era of space telescopes, this question is ever so more exciting.

Check out my iPoster and article in the SPS Observer here!

Brown Dwarfs

My research journey officially kick-started in September 2021, when I joined Dr. Adam Burgasser’s Cool Star Lab at UC San Diego. Here, I was introduced to techniques in observational astronomy — coding, reading literature, and interacting in group meetings.

I contributed to the development of the SpeX Archive database and later returned to the lab in 2024 to analyze JWST spectra of Y dwarfs!
